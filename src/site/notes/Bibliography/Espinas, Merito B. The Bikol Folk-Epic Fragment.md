_**Ibálong**_

Ilíng

Kadungung, kindly tell us all

About the old times of Handyóng,

Sing with your lyre of silver tone

The sweet enchantment of Aslóng


Nobody else can put in song

More exquisitely than you know

The mysteries that not enshroud

This glorious land of long ago.


Sing, then, and tell us of its kings,

Their proud past and their valiant role

In war which they fought on until

They had defeated Old Oryól.


Do tell us also about

The story of the old Asóg,

Of the still young Masaragá,

And of the ancient Isaróg.


Among those who had seen the lake

In which was buries Fair Takáy,

You were the tender, sweetest, most

Enticing bard to strum a sigh.


Then, sing, for we shall listen to

Your lovely song attentively

Out in the shade, right where we sit,

Here underneath the _daó_ tree.


Your sons of Bikol, listen then,

At once responded Kadungung,

To the great deeds in this old clime,

This lovely homeland of Handyóng.


The Kabikolan is a realm

Of wide plains and of fertile fields,

The fairest in what nature gives,

The richest in what planting yields.


Baltóg was the first hero known

To live upon this lovely place,

He was of Botavara land

And of Lipód, which was his race.


He scoured the region once, because

A boar of great ferocity

Destroyed his _linsa_ plants once night

And he pursued it furiously.


When he had cornered it, Baltóg

Threw down his lance, then with his bare

Herculean arms he broke its jaws

In combat that was truly fair.


Both arms extended was the length

Of its jawbones and, at a glance,

Its curling tusks were two-thirds of

The handle of his mighty lance.


When he went back to his estate

He hung the jawbones on a tree,

A big _talisay_ near his house,

In Old Tondól for all to see.


Old hunters even were awestruck

To see such wonders chase could bring

Such glorious trophies of the hunt

Brought home by Great Baltóg, their king.


And people coming from the tribes

Of Panikwason and Asóg

Said in their days saw not a boar

As bis as that killed by Baltóg.


They called it the Tandayag boar

Of Lingyón Hill that loomed ahead,

Because it was exactly like

The monster Béhemot, they said.


Soon after this to Bikol came

The Chief Handyóng, who with his band

Of warriors, in no time destroyed

All the wild creatures of the land.


To kill all these wild animals

In many thousand fights fought he,

In all of which, despite the odds,

He had emerged triumphantly.


The beasts with one eye and three throats

Which, in Ponóng, had made their lair,

Ten months without rest he attacked

Till he had slain them all right there.


The winged sharks that ploughed the deep,

The carabaos in their wild style

That roamed the rugged mountainsides

Were all subdued in a short while.


All the gigantic crocodiles

The size of bancas today

And all the fierce _sarimáo_

To Kulasi exiled away.


The serpents with the voice much like

That of a siren’s tempting call,

At Mount Hantík, in a huge cave,

They were forever buried all.


But one he could not just defeat

Despite all cunning that was done,

It was the old sagacious snake

Oryól, the most elusive one.


Oryól was wiser than Handyóng

Whose human eyes it led to see

As if it were a sweet young thing

Beckoning him seductively.


Handyóng bound it a thousand ways

Which it but scoffed at mockingly,

It easily untied the knots,

Because of its sagacity.


With sweet words, many times it fooled

Handyóng, and were it but to cheat,

None was a worse pretended than

Oryól, the champion of deceit.


How many times, without a rest,

He searched to woods, for as it were,

He thought that what had lured him on

Was the voice of a siren there!


The tasks and gains of Hercules,

They just would not have been at all

Had there been an interference

From such a creature as Oryól.


But acting inconsistently

Oryól itself did help Handyóng

To conquer all the monsters that

Have infested the region long.


Together, hand-in-hand, they fought

The giant crocodiles, and won,

And from these deadly combats he

Came out unhurt to carry on.


The _pongos_ and orangutans

Watching him fight were filled with dread,

For with the blood of crocodiles

He tinged the Bikol River red.


Those wild apes which were troublesome,

Whose bravery was widely spread,

The huge Handyóng drove all away

To Isaróg where they had fled.


Now with the land set free at last

With no more beasts of prey to kill,

Handyóng then thought of making laws

To serve his people better still.


And on a hill, he and his friends,

They planted _linsa_, which were all

So big their tuber roots were as

The giant size of a _pansól.
_

Then also in a lowland site

Rich-yielding rice they sowed along,

The kind that was for centuries

Known by the nickname _hinandyóng.
_

The first to build a boat to ply

The Bikol River was Handyóng,

Except its rudder and its sail

Which were the works of Kimantóng.


Kimantóng did invent the plow,

The harrow and the roll in tow,

The _ganta_, other measures, too,

The yoke, the bolo, and the hoe.


The loom and bobbins had Hablóm

Worked hard to make, yet these one day

To the surprise of everyone,

To Chief Handyóng he gave away.


The jar, the clay pot and the stove,

The earthen bowl, you would recall,

And other wares, the pygmy called

Dináhong had invented all.


Surál had curiously combined

A syllabary he ccarved on

A stone from Libong, and which rock

Was polished later by Gapón.


They built a town with houses done

In various sizes up along

And hanging from the branches of

The _banasi_ and _kamagóng._


With swarming insects all around

And with the burning sun to beat,

It was up the _moóg_ alone

That one could go to bear the heat.


Just laws Handyóng had made for all,

Their life and honor to protect—

A firm equality to which

The high and low were both subject.


Each to his own position kept,

The slave and master to each showed

Respect for rights of heritage

And of succession as bestowed.


Then came a deluge on the land

Caused by the Onós force of old

So that the features of this earth

Were changed completely to behold.


Volcanoes Hantík, Isaróg,

Kulasi burst eruptively,

And simultaneously was felt

The whole ground quake convulsively.


The earth thus jolted forcefully

To its bottom the sea gave way

Causing a strip of land to rise

At Pasakáw as seen today.


A torn part of the mainland formed

The islet known as Malbogóng

Where went to live the two witches

Whose names were Hilang and Laryóng.


The Inarihan overflowed,

Its course due East ran up all wrong,

So that before this wild event

It poured its waters to Ponóng.


In Bató a big mountain sank

And in its old place not for long,

A lake came up which now supplies

With fish the whole of Ibálong.


From the gulf of Kalabangan

Were wiped out all the Dagátnong,

From which had come the Dumagat

Who had inhabited Kotmóng.


And soon the land grew powerful

In the brave times of Young Bantóng

Who was a close companion of

The then war-tested Old Handyóng.


Handyóng gave him a thousand men

To kill the wild Rabót as planned,

Who was half-human and half-beart,

A liar that had plagued the land.


All the brave men, before Bantóng,

Who dared to near its lair at least

Had turned at once to sold stones

By the sheer magic of the beast.


But smart Bantóng had rightly guessed

That this Rabót slept heavily

And that it did this all day long

By simply sprawling heedlessly.


One floody day, watched by his men,

Bantóng went on, his job to do,

Before Rabót could rise, Bantóng

Had cut its body into two.


Then rose a loud and happy shout

From the bold warriors of Bantóng

That rang throughout the mangrove swamps,

The _bunga_ and _kamagóng.
_

The corpse brought to Ligmanan was

Viewed closely by the Great Handyóng,

It so unnerved him that he stayed

Shocked very terribly and long.


For surely he had never seen

A more atrocious living thing

With form more grossly horrible

And voice much louder than its ring.


Kadungung at this point stopped

The first part of his ancient lay

To leave its continuation to

The leisure of another day.
