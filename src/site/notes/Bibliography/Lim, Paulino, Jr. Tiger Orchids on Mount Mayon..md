**Motifs**

1. Past volcanic activity
    
    1. 1814 eruption
        
    2. “Once again, as it did two years ago, Mayon Volcano threatens the land that it graces with its beauty. Not all the roads destroyed in that eruption have been reparied; the train still can’t go to Legazpi because the bridge in the barrio of Cagsawa has not been rebuilt. The ricefields covered by lava will remain barren for many years to come.” - spoken by Fr Pelagio, p. 30
        
    3. “Zenaida, do you see that tour bus near the ruins?”
        
        She saw the dark tower and the lights below it.
        
        “Must be exciting to watch the fireworks from the ruins of another eruption.” - James to Zenaida, p. 63
        
2. Flowers/Orchids
    
    1. “He picks up an orchid, gathering its knife-like leaves around the pedicel, and set it in the husk, the roots balled in dried banana stalk; then he filled the sides with moss and crushed bark before letting the leaves fan out. A green and yellow receptacle, shaped like a slipper toe, topped the pedicel behind three green sepals cradling a whorl of bright yellow petals spotted with brown.
        
        He lifted the coconut husk and slowly turned, seeing the orchid against the darkened doorway, thatched roof and ashen afternoon light. It was too beautiful to sell, but he needed the money.” - p. 25
        
    2. “There are those who will claim that the eruption is the natural result of pressure that has built up below the earth’s crust. Others will see the eruption as an omen, a portent of violence that will engulf the land. Still others will take it as the price we pay for the volcano’s gifts to us, our rich agriculture, the firewood and flowers from its forests, **the beautiful orchids that decorate our gardens**. And many others, I am sure, will see the eruption as an indication that God is punishing us for our sins and warning us to repent before it is too late.” - spoken by Fr Pelagio, p. 30
        
    3. potted epiphyllum - p. 46, p. 90
        
    4. “There’s the cymbidium tiger orchid. Look at the flowers, eight on one spike.”
        
        “Beautiful.”
        
        “They’re not as beautiful as the tiger orchids on Mayon.”
        
        “Oh? I’ve heard about them but have never really seen any.”
        
        “Those flowers are tiny. Pretty brown spots but the yellow is dull. The tiger orchids on Mount Mayon are bigger, up to seven inches across. The Bengal of tiger orchids.”
        
        “Don’t you have a Mayon tiger orchid?”
        
        “No, I’ve only seen it once.”
        
        “You’ve got quite a collection here.” ”Without flowers the orchid looks like potted grass, but when it blooms, nothing in man’s work or nature can compare.”
        
        Father Pelagio laughed; he had not seen Manolo so animated.
        
        “This is one of my favorites. Java _phaelenopsis amabilis_—beautiful name, isn’t it?”
        
        Father Pelagio peered at the blossom, three white petals and red labellum.
        
        “And this is the one I’ve been watching. See this tiny raceme shooting out from the base of the bulb? Flowers will grow on the raceme and I’d better be around when it blooms.”
        
        “Why?”
        
        “The flower blooms for only six to seven minutes.”
        
        “My God!”
        
        “It blooms like a cloud of butterflies, then wilts. Think of it, Father, a flower that blooms only for as long as it takes you to consecrate the host t Mass before it dies.”
        
        “I don’t think of flowers as dying. I’ve seen children die, men and women in their prime, but I just don’t see flowers as dying.”
        
        “This orchid makes me think of death.” - Manolo and Fr Pelagio in conversation, p. 48-49
        
    5. Manolo appeared, trowel in hand.
        
        “It’s awful, Father, just awful.”
        
        “Why, Manolo, what happened?”
        
        “I missed the amabilis.”
        
        “The what?”
        
        “The amabilis orchid. It bloomed last night and it’s already wilted. Like a coward, afraid of the flood, I ran to the church.” - Manolo to Fr. Pelagio, p. 82
        
    6. “How about your tiger orchids?”
        
        “New buds are coming out. I hope the flowers will be prettier than the ones I showed you.”
        
        “Why, because of the eruption?”
        
        “No, not this one. That’s only true for tiger orchids that grow on Mount Mayon.” - Fr. Pelagio to Manolo, p. 82

3. Religion
    
    1. John 21: 1-19 - Fr Pelagio’s sermon, p. 29
    2. Baptism: “Of course, as baptisms go, by fire, water, or ashes, who knows? A rebirth, whatever that means. It’s supposed to change your spiritual nature. I’m not sure whether the child has nature to speak of.” - James to Marjorie, p. 54
    3. Procession: “Oh, a procession. We do it when Mayon is erupting. We pray the rosary, sign hymns and ask sinners to repent.” - Zenaida to Marjorie, p. 67
    4. “Should he pray that the ash blow away from the procession? Another frivolous thought. Even the songs from the booklet did not ask for so specific a favor. The vernacular lyrics exhorted the sleeping Christians to wake up, meditate on their sins and ask the Blessed Mother to intercede for them. The Spanish lyrics addressed directly to God begged forgiveness for sins. No word at all about being spared the volcano’s fire and flood, only about being spiritually ready for death.” - p. 75
    5. “If sins were forgiven, atoned for by the midnight ritual of procession, there was no need for punishment. But if it came, flood or fire, it would still be in keeping with the same theology of evil that accounted for one’s drowning in Noah’s deluge, turning into a pillar of salt, and being waved out of a garden abode by and angel’s flaming sword. The hymn writers knew; lava flow was directed by gravity, hence, no hymn or prayer attempted to influence divine will, but the faithful, reminded of sin and punishment by hell fire spewing from the volcano, could at least change their way, accept their plight of poverty, disease, and old age.” - p. 76
4. Dissent/Revolution
    
    1. Mark being approached to conduct “teach-in” classes in the mountains, as a guise to join the revolution
        
    2. “He’s trying. At least he didn’t dwell on Mayon’s eruption as God’s warning to us. Look at that volcano. We hardly notice it. Now that it’s belching and raining ash, we’re all scared. What can the priest really say about it?”
        
        “Give a lesson in geology or speak out against graft.”
        
        “You do listen to his sermon.”
        
        “I’m not the only one. I hear the military is listening. But Father hasn’t yet given a sermon calling for the removal of American bases.”
        
        “He may be what they call a soft-core revolutionary.”
        
        “And what’s hardcore?”
        
        “Well, those ten or more priests who have joined the rebel forces.”
        
        “I see. Wasn’t a priest killed while leading guerrillas in a battle with government troops?”
        
        “Yes, Father Agatep. The Church has begun a crackdown, as the papers put it. I’m surprised neither Father Pelagio or Marquez has talked about the bishops’ pastoral letter warning priests and nuns to sever links with the communists.”
        
        “Why didn’t the Church do it earlier?”
        
        “Denounce the radical priests while still under martial law? No way. The Church wanted to get rid of Marcos as much as the communists did,” Mark said. - Mark and Zenaida in conversation, p. 33-34
        
    3. “James shook his head, followed Mark’s gaze as he stared at Mayon, wondering at how he could make light of a threat to his life. Perhaps it was like the way the people were taking the eruption of Mayon. Laugh it off and wait for what tomorrow would bring.” - p. 44-45
        
5. Fatalism?
    
6. “The people did not seem to mind, assuming a wait-and-see attitude, a native trait he was told of a country given to fatalism. He had heard no talk about evacuation. The eruption was intermittent, signalled by quakes, explosion, pyroclastics in the air, then the molten lava bubbling over the crater and flowing down the ravines with tounges of fire.” - p. 41
    
7. “Of course. A volcano-watching party. Marjorie says that a volcanic eruption has the same principle as unpopping a bottle of champagne.” - James, to Zenaida, p. 42
