In the winding alleyways that run alongside the homes in Barangay Cobo, one might hear the sharp, shrill ring of metal grating against a stone polish. Here in Cobo, fire bows to the swift and sure movements of the pandays who know best to tame it.

Blacksmithing in Cobo began in the early 1800s and surged in the 1940s when the Philippine National Railway (PNR) project fell through. The railroad tracks that had been in operation since the early 1900s were now considered useless and fair game, as Tabaquenos began stripping the metal from the ground. This would fuel the reinvigoration of the blacksmithing culture in Tabaco. Blacksmiths from the nearby barangays of Baranghawon and Tayhi soon migrated to Cobo and established pandayans there. In *Stop-Over: Tabaco* ^[1], Mariano L. Kilates, contemplating questions of citihood in 1972, talks about the export of Tabaqueño products like metalcraft and abaca. He attempts to trace the history of metalcraft and notes that Tabaco may have been known for metalcraft before the Spaniards came. As the local economy rose, the city's scissors and blades began to be exported as far as Germany.

Today, only a handful of workshops remain. From 40 pandayans, only a few remain in operation. Speaking with the barangay captain, I learn that many of the pandayans have closed up shop since they are now able to send their children to school. Blacksmithing is a job that is passed down, but in the way that the craft was passed down to them by their fathers, they would rather their children look for more stable jobs. Many of the pandays lack formal education, having trained from childhood, pounding at metal everyday until they attain mastery of the fire.

New threats emerge: there is great competition with cheaply produced goods coming from China. Perhaps efficiently produced, but of lesser quality, I am assured by the barangay captain. There is also the waning interest in handling the hammer, as children are preferring to focus on their formal studies and office jobs. The raw material is also now more difficult to source. Many pandays in the barangay source their scrap metal from junkyards in Metro Manila, shipped to Bicol to be turned into tabaks and scissors.

The products that come out of the pandayan are world-class. They may discolor over time, but remain sharp even after decades. When my grandmother passed away during the pandemic, and my mother finally decided to clear out my lola’s house, I immediately went to the tiny cupboard by the stairs. This was where my grandmother kept her sewing materials, away from the Singer sewing machine that she kept covered on the second floor. She used to sew dresses for my dolls, each one labor of care and a source of fascination for me, seeing the little lace-lined clothes. In the cupboard lay my lola’s s aspili scissors, rusted and discolored, the metal oxidized from years of use. I tested them out on an empty envelope that was wedged in between the book that lay on the shelf. They snipped right through.

The blades of Tabaco are sharp. They glimmer in the market and earn character with time. In some households, they are passed down through generations, if not given as gifts. They speak of the city itself, with the fire that galvanizes these tools, the heat from one’s hand too oxidizes the metal, marking each blade with time and use. And as these pandayans face extinction, perhaps it mirrors, too, the city and its treatment of history. The very name of Tabaco originates from the signature blades crafted in the heat of these pandayans, and these workshops might be gone soon. The irony is stark.

The barangay captain notes that the LGU hopes to bolster support for the blacksmiths. A building had been promised: one that would serve as a center of trade, containing old and new equipment that the pandays could train on and hopefully incorporate into their production process. The goal is to streamline production and to take a step toward professionalizing the craft. The barangay holds onto this promise, as it might keep the fires burning in the pandayans, their homes, the barangay, and ultimately, the city.
