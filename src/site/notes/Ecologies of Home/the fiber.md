Abaca, or manila hemp, is a fiber harvested from a plant in the banana family. It is stripped, sun-dried, and, should you please, spun into ropes that are then used to weave baskets and bags. The fiber is hardy, rough to the touch, and more pliable than others. It is versatile too, as it can be used for clothing (typically through sinamak preparation), baskets, mats, farming materials, and cordage.

Shinzo Hayase[^1] writes about the history of abaca production, popularized in the Spanish colonial period and industrialized under the Americans. By then, abaca plantations had been established in the Bicol and Davao regions. Production and export grew steadily, such that in 1904, Manila hemp accounted for 72% of the country’s total export value, declining only after World War I, when it was reduced to 40%. The Bicol region remained the primary producer of Manila hemp until the Japanese introduced modern plantation management strategies to Davao.

Bicol’s humid climate and high instance of rainfall proved productive for cultivating abaca. The soil, too, was made even richer by the Mayon Volcano. But there was Davao, with the rich terroir of Mount Apo, and without the typhoons that frequently raged in Bicol. Decades later, in the second quarter of 2023, the Bicol Region was once more listed by the Philippine Statistics Authority as the top producer of abaca in the country.[^2]

Currently, one of the biggest companies for abaca production is Ching Bee Trading Corporation. It was founded in the 1940s in Tabaco, Albay by Mr. Lim Ching Bee, and currently has buying stations and branches in Tabaco City, Baybay City, Leyte, and Davao City. It exports abaca globally and transports the fiber to Japan, the US, and Europe.[^3] As of 2025, the Bicol Region is listed as one of the few areas where abaca farming, pulp processing, and cordage manufacturing is concentrated. According to the same report, most the the processed abaca products, "particularly abaca pulp (78.6%), cordage/rope (4.9%), and fibercraft/fabrics (0.9%), fabrics and yarns (0.7%) raw fiber (14.9%) are exported abroad. About 97.0% of the total abaca production in the country is exported which generated an equivalent of more than Php8 billion revenue in 2019."^[4] Contesting the colonially rooted myth of the "lazy native," Norman G. Owen in *Prosperity Without Progress* contends that it is precisely this bias toward exporting abaca that has, as early as the twentieth century, led to the rise in abaca prices and, subsequently, the overall destabilizing of the market. ^[5]

For the people of Tabaco, abaca exists simply as abaca. The public market crawls with fiber and its products. Abaca exists as a readymade, as bags and home decor – the usual pasalubong. These are familiar sights. At some point, I even grew incredulous of people using these fiber bags in the metro when it became trendy to use them.

In the market, there are those who have passed three days without selling a thing. Handicrafts sales have withered in the last few years. The more open market of today has made these products more accessible.

“Sa bundok,” my parents would say, the same when I ask them where the abaca is made. It is the same thing uttered by the marketfolk. In the neighboring municipality of Malilipot (malipot being the Bikol word for “cold”), the people gather every July to celebrate the Lubid Festival. Malilipot, like Tabaco, sits near the coast and at the foot of mountains. In these mountains, not far from the sentro of Malilipot, one will find a small community of manlulubid (rope-weavers.)

Finding them was also not an easy task. Having passed many houses, the residents simply scratched their heads at the mention of manlulubid. “Warara na man digdi,” said one of the people we passed by on the road. There are none here anymore. That the manlulubid would be gone seemed absurd, considering how the town festival was built on these lubid. But this too was what I found in Tabaco, so perhaps there is a quiet loss that unfolds in these Albay towns.

We continue up the winding road, through the trees, past a dam, and up to the very end of the paved road onto which the mountain seemed to close in. Here, we met a man who had instructed us to trek up an incline toward a clearing. Amid the trees, he said, we would find the only manlulubid working on a rainy December morning. “Amay yan nagtatrarabaho,” he said. And never on rainy days. But this one family working in the clearing had a makeshift tent. It was about a 20-meter tarpaulin propped up on thick branches stuck in the ground.

Under this tent were two groups spinning fiber into ropes. One person would operate the wheel – a contraption made from the hubcap of a bicycle. A handle was attached to the side, allowing the person to spin the wheel easily by hand. It was propped up on a wooden stand. Away from the wheel, at every meter-and-a-half intervals, Y-shaped branches were stuck in the ground. These branches went on for about 20 meters. Pairs of workers gracefully but swiftly set laid loosely bundled abaca fibers on the branches. Once they reached the end, they would tie the ends together. The spinner needed no signal; they knew when to turn the wheel. The fibers would then tighten, swiftly spun into the familiar lubid. The process takes about ten minutes. At the end of it, the pairs would help bundle and stack the ropes, after which the process is repeated.

Each kilo costs 25 pesos – “sa labor sana,” because the materials are not theirs, they remind me – just the mechanism and the place. It is the same arrangement for the ones harvesting the raw abaca. But lately, even gathering the raw fiber has grown more difficult. The recent eruptions have made many areas inaccessible, limiting what artisans are able to harvest. The production of some merchandise has halted because of this. The market vendors tell me that the rains do not affect them, but the volcano does.

Today, abaca exists as one in a long line of statistics in our export economy. It offers stability and imposes placelessness–far from what I encountered in the mountains. In the uplands, volcanic unrest and heavy rainfall determine a family’s capacity to rest easy. How telling too that our artisans are unrecognized in their own communities. As the money passes through foreign and powerful hands, how much of it really benefits the people on the ground?
