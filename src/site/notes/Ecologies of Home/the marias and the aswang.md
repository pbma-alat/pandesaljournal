[[Ecologies of Home/By Flood and Fire - Ecologies of Home\|By Flood and Fire - Ecologies of Home]]

#ecofeminism #geomythology #fireecology #ecologiesofhome #angelamanalanggloria #citrasasmita #nuestraseñora #womeninmythology #bicolmythology #aswang 

In the works of Balinese artist [[Artists/Citra Sasmita\|Citra Sasmita]], fire engulfs women. Tongues of flame orbit the figures as the conflagration coats their bodies, falling, most significantly, on the womb. In this geomythological conception of women, they exist with fire, their naked bodies coextensive with sacred flame while refusing sartorial regime. In her works, Sasmita challenges Balinese traditions codified in mythology and folklore by instilling women with agency marked by the feminine divine. In doing so, she articulates her own cosmology where women--sometimes depicted as snakes (*Fragments from Book of Fire 1, 2024*), other times as a totem of decapitated heads, each one with a spring of blood emerging from the mouth (*Peculiar Garden, 2022*)--embody the heroism historically attributed to males. The women of Sasmita's universe embrace the body as a vessel of personal memory and colonial violence, raising the wounded corporeal entity as a force of nature. In an interview with KADIST, she states, **“A woman’s body is a record of the experiences and realities of life that women experience and how they live their lives both in nature and social construction.”**[^1]

Bicolano folklore begins with ~~fire~~ a woman. [[Ecologies of Home/the volcano\|The volcano]] is a woman. The river is a woman. So too are the serpent[^2], the Mother, and the whore. In popular and folkloric imagination, women are recast as vessels of rage and care. And much like fire, the woman is revered, desired, feared, and ultimately regulated. As in the violent histories that Sasmita wrestles with, these portrayals are shaped by dimensions of power, as the female becomes the subject of masculine feuds and social judgment, all while enduring the weight of colonial projections.

A cursory look at Bicolano mythology suggests that the women exist as the lesser deities. The sky is governed by the male god Languiton, the waters and the celestial ocean by Tubigan, the wind by Paros, fishing by Maguindang, destruction by Kalaon, and the forest by Okot. [^3] The god of deluge, Onos, is male. Gugurang, the supreme deity, is also male.

Meanwhile, the female appears as the serpent Oryol, daughter of Asuang, who uses her beauty to deceive and seduce men. ~~The female is the buruka, the indigenous bruja or witch.~~ She is the naga with their silver eel-like tails, not to be confused with the flesh-eating magindara whose tails flare in the colors of the rainbow.[^5] Across these portrayals, female beauty acts as a counterpart to her tragedy. [^6]

In the human realm, women chiefly served as mediums between the material and the supernatural worlds. The baliana were described as the “most shrewd, libertine, and seductive,”[^11] even as they officiated rituals for the tribe. They acted as exorcists,  casting away the asuang believed to possess the sick and bring famine to the lands. The feast of Haliya was also performed by the baliana. It was meant to revere the goddess of the moon as she battled Bakonawa, the sky-serpent.[^4] Men existed as spiritual guides within these tribes, too. They were known to dress up as women, performing the same rituals as the balyana.

Jesus Cyril M. Conde[^12], in considering this link between the asog and baliana, offers a different reading of oneness to the tale of Oryol. His argument, however, is written with the unfortunate anchoring on the male counterpart of Handyong and the asog. Conde suggests that Handyong and Oryol mirror the alliance between the asog ang the baliana, ultimately evidencing the oneness of genders in precolonial Bicol. Yet an examination of the characterization of the serpent-maiden -- deceitful, inconsistent, seductive, and elusive and only wise insofar as she serves the warrior -- reveals the clear inequality of this dynamic. Sasmita gestures to a similar tension in Balinese folklore, and it would not be strange to find the same themes emerge in Filipino folklore.

In this essay, I contend that the wounding of the female in myth and in material reality all at once subdues and completes her. In offering this eco-feministic reading of selected texts, we might begin to locate an understanding of how particular worlds are experienced and shaped by the female, and vice-versa.

**Asuang and the Stolen Flame**

In one version of the myth of Mayon, fire becomes the object of desire for the greedy Asuang. Asuang steals the fire kept by Gugurang in the depths of Mayon Volcano, after first asking for a share of the fire and being refused. In anger, Gugurang, in anger, begins his lament and rebuke against Asuang, exclaiming, "You! You lay god! Look at your ragged mountain and compare it to Mayon which is the most beautiful in the world." In response, Asuang sends down spirits to influence the people toward evil.

	"How dare you ask for my fire!" Gugurang thundered. The earth trembled. "Don't you know that when the fire in my seat is carried by hands such as yours the whole world will be set on fire?"
	
	"But I will be very careful," replied Asuang.
	
	"Be careful! I myself with all my power cannot handle it."
	
	"But how can you threaten the people with it?"
	
	"It is not my will that does it. It is someone else's that you or I do not know nor will ever know."
	
	"But the rainy days are coming and I need fire to make me warm in Mount Malinao."
	
	"Why," answered Gugurang, "you have lived there for many years and this is the first time that you have asked me for it, what will you use the fire for? Look at your people; they can live without it."
	
	"Well, it is time for you to give them fire now."
	
	"Give them fire!" burst Gugurang. The earth shook and the people were more afraid. But soon Gugurang quelled the commotion. Asuang himself was frightened. He never saw him that way before. "They are not fit to have it yet! They must make themselves worthy."
	
	"Well, am I not worthy?"
	
	"You! you lay god! Look at your ragged mountain and compare it to Mayon which is the most beautiful in the world."

In the pantheon of Bicolano gods, Asuang is categorically defined as male. Here, however, I offer a speculative reading in which Asuang, recalling the colonial demonification of the generic asuang, is understood as female. Read in this way, the story of Asuang and Gugurang delivers a sharp warning against female rebellion, revealed through the declarations made by Gugurang. In the heat of their argument, Asuang challenges the rule of Gugurang. “But between us two, there is not much difference. Why must I live in a humble place like Mount Malinao while you sit here gloating over your power unlimited and unchecked?” The supreme deity's preoccupation with beauty and hierarchy felt notably unquestioned.

The story culminates in the disfigurement of Mount Malinao, the home of Asuang. A figure of abject horror, the withered and tragic Aswang typically functions as a foil to the beauty and gallantry of mythic protagonists. Mythology positions the two gods on the opposing ends of good and evil, and this divide is galvanized when Asuang steals the fire from the heart of Mayon. As the flame is taken out of the volcano, the lands are engulfed in a blazing inferno. Gugurang pursues and eventually subdues Asuang, reclaiming the fire. At the conclusion of this godly battle, the humans end up keeping fragments of the fallen embers, which keep them warm in the deluge of rain sent by Gugurang. It is in this way that Asuang, the rageful villain, inadvertently fulfills an act of kindness for the people of Albay.  In this story, power is not granted, but seized. Most notably, while Gugurang's fire functions as a disciplinary tool, Asuang's fire marks the rupture of the moral divide.
 
The commong aswang is often read alongside its female nature. The aswang is the female transmogrified, with origins tracing back to when the babaylan and the revolutionary were scornfully recast as monstrous under colonial and patriarchal regimes. Herminia Meñez suggests that the perversion of asuang emerged as a colonial phenomenon during the Spanish era, as part of a broader effort to extinguish indigenous animist practices that were often embodied by the baliana or babaylan.

In a 1983 text, Raul Pertierra links the "asuangification" of women and the feminization of the asuang to women's refusal to observe codified norms between the sexes, even as they continued to occupy spaces socially delegated to women. He writes, "The notion of the asuang represents a denial of basic sociality and a refusal to recognize reciprocity and exchange. Asuangs are dangerous not only because they deny the basis of society but also because they intrude into its innermost recesses." Thus, for friars who represented colonial authority, the supplanting of female shamanistic powers and sexual force and the simultaneous forcing of women into a role of monstrosity was necessary to maintaining moral order in the patriarchal society.

In Philippine urban legend, Maria Labo represents a modern telling of this inversion. The story of Maria Labo, the overseas-Filipino-worker-turned-aswang, began circulating in the 1990s in Negros as a figure of  horror. Maria Labo seeks justice for the defilement of her body by violent men. Her story centers on the brutal wounding of the female body, but it also considers how Maria Labo's narrative is equally shaped by the harm thrust upon her by society and circumstance. She starts out as an OFW who is cursed with the vampiric power of her dying employer. Upon her return, she succumbs to her craving for human flesh and eventually kills and cooks her two sons. Her husband later discovers the horrific scene and begins to slash at his wife with a bolo. Maria Labo escapes, but is said to still carry the large gash across her face as she searches for her next meal.

Maria Labo's story offers a clear cautionary tale for OFWs, yet the repeated positioning of female body as the site of violence reinforces a broader pattern. Eisa Jocson writes, “Maria Labo embodies female rage. She represents revolution and subversion, summoning us to break out of our subservient Mary-fied shell and unleash our transfigured rage. It is after all, captured in the meaning of her name, Maria Slashed; the task is to hack open Maria. Her wound is a portal to our depths and a crack to unleash what has been repressed.”[^6] Crucially, it is this wound that empowers and completes Maria Labo. Like the fracturing of Asuang’s Mount Malinao and the transformation of Magayon, these figures converge as injured landscapes and defiled bodies, articulating the connections of woman, myth, and nature.

The story of Asuang and Gugurang offers little kindness to the former, as the moral remains: female rebellion results in ruin. Asuang is cast as an insubordinate thief who disrupts the balance that demands to remain unquestioned. They are depicted as inferior and ends up less, as their home, Mount Malinao, is cut in half. In this condemnation, the tension persists. It is the very act of transgression that marks Asuang as monstrous, but it, too, enables survival. The stolen fire, used by Gugurang to punish, ultimately becomes a gift.

**Magayon, the Maiden**

The legend of Daragang Magayon follows a familiar tropism. Magayon is placed at the center of the conflict between Pangaronon, her lover, and Pagtuga, a scorned admirer. When Pangaronon dies, Magayon takes her own life. In the legend, Magayon stands as the nexus of love, battle, and grief. Buried by her father, Makusog, her body eventually rises from the earth as the Mayon Volcano.

In the Bicolano imagination, Mayon is a resplendent marker of abundance, beauty, and home, but Mayon is also powerful and volatile. She is at once maiden and princess, unswayed by the laurels and gifts of her suitors, yet ultimately trapped in the trope of the grief-stricken lover. Her love works against her: her father is abducted by a jealous Pagtuga, her lover perishes in a battle for her affection, and, in a world without Pangaronon, she swiftly takes a knife and stabs herself in the heart. In fearlessly choosing love, Magayon inevitably chooses death and yields to the loss of self-determination. Magayon is not cast as monstrous like the demonic asuang, but her story nevertheless returns the female body to ruin. Her legend unfolds as further mythical evidence of the connection between woman and nature.

A similar trope may be found in the legend of Takay, associated with the water hyacinth found in Lake Buhi, Camarines Sur. Her story appears briefly in the epic Ibalong:

	Among those who had seen the lake
	In which was buried Fair Takay,

Some versions suggest that Onos, god of storms and deluge, had become enamored with Takay since she was a young girl. Bulan had warned Onos against desiring a human, but the latter did not listen. When Onos proclaimed his love to Takay, she rejected him in favor of her lover, a lad named Kanaway. In anger, Onos struck Kanaway and turned him to stone. He then sent down lightning upon the lands, flooding Mount Asog and forming Lake Buhi. Haliya and Bulan were unable to stop the angered god. Upon their descent ot earth, they see the flooded lands and the drowned people of Ibalong. Among the dead was the fair maden Takay. Moved by pity, Bulan transformed her lifeless body into the lovely white flower that rests on the lake today.

These mythic violences are not isolated as we are able to locate some resonance in historical systems of control. In his book entitled *Caliban and the Witch*, Silvia Federici explores how the rise of capitalism required control of the land and, by extension, control of bodies. This articulation of control was meant to strangle the female-assigned processes of reproduction and gestures of care, which the patriarchal capitalistic  order had deemed unproductive. For Chris J. Cuomo, writing in *Feminism and Ecological Communities*, this control extends into the gendering of intellectual domains. It is no surprise, he explains, that men continue to dominate the hard sciences and other disciplines relating to the natural or material world. The broader alignment between masculinity, knowledge, and authority persists even in this manner.

Federici points out that women accused of witchcraft were reduced to the destruction of their bodies, particularly by fire. This is a suffering inflicted by the same patriarchal society that cast women as inherently sinful. Kathleen Nadeau makes a powerfully succint statement in her essay entitled *Aswang and Other Kinds of Witches: A Comparative Analysis*: "The real fear of the Europeans may be based on the fear of uncontrollable women." In the same essay, she discusses the relegation of women, who were once central to the spiritual life of a community, to the domestic enclosure. This is further complicated and given structure by the gendered dynamic with the male husband, whose life existed publicly and whose actions always transpired in systems of reciprocity. In contrast, the female refused reciprocity. In the occasion that it did happen, it does so privately or implicitly. Here, Nadeau draws a parallel between the woman and the asuang. The latter, too, resists reciprocity. As a monstrous entity, the asuang exists as an asocial figure who completely denies all social relations.

Women’s proximity to nature was likewise scorned, though many accusations of witchcraft only emerged years later, as the capitalist system deemed natural and paganistic practices to be unproductive. The figure most often targeted was the old woman who existed in close relation to nature, memory, and embodied knowledge. Magayon, like the witch, interfaces with multiple conceptions of the female. She exists in a complex bond with nature, further complicated through patriarchal intervention.

The connection between woman and nature, then, is neither mythic nor mystical, but patriarchal and systemic. As Karen J. Warren (1994) observes, “the exploitation of nature and animals is justified by feminizing them; the exploitation of women is justified by naturalizing them.” Fire, in the mythological and folkloric narratives discussed, significantly emerges as both power and punishment, reflecting the same logic of domination.

**Devotion and Disaster**

Elsewhere, in the iconography of Nuestra Señora de Salvacion, we find a different configuration of fire. Fire in this space surfaces as an aspect of divinity, but nevertheless allows us to further draw out the relationship between women and nature.

Canonically crowned the patroness of Albay province on August 25, 1976, Nuestra Señora de Salvacion offers a complex interplay of fire and the female. This Marian image contains a curious iconography that some churches have deemed perturbing. The Virgin Mary carries the Child Jesus in her left arm while reaching out to save a man stumbling backward. Below the man is the devil’s head which, in multiple depictions, is shown with fire spewing from the mouth of the grotesque figure. On the other side of Mary, a repentant sinner offers up a basket of flaming hearts. In this considerable configuration of figures, the Mother, the Child, the penitent, the rescued, and the demon altogether narrate Albay's story of salvation, underscoring its strong belief in divine protection while conversing with its histories of disaster and material conditions.

In a small poblacion in Albay, we find another curious Marian image that speaks directly to place-based histories. The Nuestra Señora de los Desamparados, affectionately called OLA or Amaparos in communities of reverence in Manila shows the Virgin Mary carrying the Child Jesus in her left hand, as she stands on a cloud lined with roses. The Bacacay Amparos does away with the usual twelve-starred halo of the Valencian iconography, and is instead clothed in her simpler Marian blue and white garb. She reaches out her right arm to a group of three Aetas, kneeling in prayer and looking up at her. Behind them are three individuals, Spanish officials, who figure into the story of OLA’s apparition. Mayon Volcano looms in the distance, smoke rising from its crater, and effectively emplacing the sacred scene.

OLA is said to have appeared to the Aeta community who reside in the hills of Bonga. The story relays how she was saddened by the “misery and abandon” in which the community was living.[^8] Their tribe's council of elders later seek out Capitan Domingo to help consult with the Cura Parroco. Later, the Cura Parroco of Malinao, Padre Mariano B. Bañez, reassures the Capitan and his people, “Doubtless the Gugurang has seen your sufferings and heard your cries for help.” In consultation with Padre Mariano B. Bersola and his economo Padre Enrique Balana, Padre Bañez would eventually commission a local painter to create the portrait of the Virgin Mary. The artist, Quirino Sanchez, rendered the image in oil and toiled from February to March of 1901.

Fire is the language of destruction, and in the iconography of the two Marian images of Salvacion and Amparos, it also exists as an offering. Particularly in the image of Salvacion, flames figure heavily, recalling the iconography of Nuestra Señora de la Lumen de Cainta. In a painting by Fernando Amorsolo, the fiery fanged demon adopts an even more monstrous face which stands in great contrast against the serene disposition of de la Lumen.

The image of Salvacion was carved in the 1770s from the trunk of a felled calpi tree in one of the estates of Don Siliverio Arcilla. Mariano Dakuba, a tenant, was tasked with clearing parts of Don Arcilla’s hacienda. In the process, Dakuba chopped down a large calpi tree that miraculously never rotted. A local sculptor named Bagacumba was eventually commissioned to carve three sacred images from the trunk, galvanizing the reverence of nature as material in the holy bodies of Our Lady of Salvation, Our Lady of Solitude, and St. Anthony of Padua. On August 25, 1776, Don Arcilla allowed for Salvacion to be installed in Joroan, which was once a vista of Buhi, Camarines Sur, upon the condition that a chapel would be built at the center of the barrio.[^9]

This story illuminated the connections between women, nature, and the sacred. Chris J. Cuomo writes, “On this view, connections between ‘woman’ and ‘nature’ exist because women are part of ‘nature,’ as are all humans, and the suppression and hatred of nature is played out in specific ways on women’s bodies, activities, and conceptual frameworks. These connections are relevant because both women and nature are categorically devalued, with their distinct and similar qualities.” (p. 7) The story of Nuestra Señora de Salvacion simultaneously subscribes to and challenges this relationship. The felling of the calpi tree can be regarded as the felling of a body, but it is here where the miracle is completed. In this space of the consecrated, Salvacion draws out the feminine divine through the process of wounding.

Similarly, similar mutilations and notably grotesque injuries inflicted upon the female body can be found in biblical narratives. These, too, lend to the fulfillment of the female saints' martyrdom. St. Wilgefortis grew a beard and was crucified by her father, St. Dearbhla plucked out her eyes to deter a suitor, St. Agatha of Sicily had her breasts cut off in an act of torture, St. Lucy’s eyes were gouged out, and St. Rose of Lima combated her own vanity by disfiguring her face with pepper and lime.

**The Poet**

Female passion and scorn are likewise captured in Angela Manalang-Gloria’s *[[Ecologies of Home/Soledad\|Soledad]]* and *[[Ecologies of Home/Revolt from Hymen\|Revolt from Hymen]]*. Her work provides us with snapshots of muddied morals - the 
"repulsive woman" - rendered through the imagery of the carceral and the violent. In both poems, she probes the shame thrust upon sacred female bodies ruined by love’s passion, consequently exposing the contentious and highly restrictive social and moral expectations placed upon women in her milieu.

*Soledad*, published in *The Philippines Herald Mid-Week Magazine* in 1935, is cloaked in fire – firebrand, flame, hell – using the imagery of its behavior – burned, aureoled, blackened – as metaphors for the unholy ruination of its protagonist. Soledad chooses love over a life expected of her, and for this, she is condemned. Through the poem, Manalang-Gloria also captures the bigoted condescension of the townspeople when stacked up against the sincerity of a girl’s love: “And seeing nothing but her blackened spires/ The town condemned this girl who loved too well.” In doing so, the poet exposes the tensions between religion and passion, virtue and constraint, the social and the personal, the patriarchal and the feminine, and delivers a critique of the agency and autonomy of the female body. Love offers condemnation and becomes grounds for punishment. Fire is present in the intensity of passion, but also in the destruction of Soledad’s name. In the end, Soledad, like her name, remains solitary.

*Revolt from Hymen*, published in 1940, intensifies this critique and further explores this matter of agency against the subject of the female body by adopting an arguably bolder approach to the subject matter. Written in the first person, the poem confronts the injuries of marriage in the brutality of marital rape. Here, desire evokes pain and disgust while solitude becomes a space of reprieve.

Stepping away from the personae in the works of Angela Manalang-Gloria, Niles Jordan Breis, in his essay, [[Kung Tawagin Siya’y Angela Buruka: Sa Alaala ni Angela Manalang-Gloria\|Kung Tawagin Siya’y Angela Buruka: Sa Alaala ni Angela Manalang-Gloria]], considers his complicated sentiments toward the poet.[^7] The poet, he relays, is remembered as a buruka, a repulsive witch, who is reviled by Tabaqueños. She loudly chastises the townspeople from the window of her large house, the former site of Bell Smith and Co., from whom the poet had wittingly bought the property at a rate much lower than market price. The poet is described as a matapobre who pours the piss from her orinola onto bystanders at the Bell Smith house. In the essay, the cruel Angela is known for her dislike of the ordinary worker, in one instance even rudely castigating a padyak driver for his stench. Manalang-Gloria becomes a caricature of a witch, but communal memory too is exposed in the essay for the perplexity of its oscillations.

Breis recalls a stern and derogatory remark made by his uncle, “Aristokrata ang hayup na burukang ‘yan. Tandaan mo na asul ang kaniyang dugo samantalang ang sa ‘yo ay pula lang. Matuto ka.” Later, as he forays into poetry, Breis is reprimanded by his teacher, “Tarantado ka palang bata ka! Pagka-buruka lang ni Angela ang alam mo. Gagaya ka pa sa mga mangmang dito sa atin na wala nang ginawa kundi libakin si Angela. Tanga!”

 Breis mentions having read the biography of Manalang-Gloria written by Edna Manlapaz yet seemingly fails to consider the conditions that led to the poet's becoming: that she lost her husband Celedonio violently, that her child was lost from her for several months, that she never considered herself nor her works good enough, that her relationship with Celedonio was filled with both love and resentment, that her life was marked by series of departures – *from her*, and that with all this, she admits to the coldness and distance that she offers her own grandchildren.
 
 Perhaps humanizing the poet should not mean reducing her to a small-town caricature of the poet mythologized as a witch. Women would benefit from being released from these reductions and roles ascribed to them. Here, I make a case for the poet who is much despised: she was neither a buruka, nor a great goddess of poetry. She simply was. Yet this world recalls most vividly the faults of women, and perhaps that is the burden that is continually placed on our backs.

The correlation that is drawn between the solitude of woman and witchlore is a phenomenon worth examining. Manalang-Gloria, like the aswang in Nadeau's account, refuses social relations. Like Maria Labo, Magayon, and the deity Asuang, her life is marked by rupture and cast with tragedies, ultimately coalescing in this resistance. It is this refusal that carries the poet into the realm of social vilification, a condition she herself has explored in her poetry.

In a conversation with her daughter, Manalang-Gloria is remembered less as a figure of myth than as a mother and grandmother—reserved, disciplined, and committed to education. She was not fond of social gatherings, preferring the quiet rhythms of domestic life. When asked how one becomes a poet, she attributed it to innate talent, invoking the enduring idea of artistic genius. Today, she is memorialized in awards and institutions, even as the preservation of her legacy—such as the restoration of the Bell Smith house—remains uncertain.

One of the poet's grandchildren recalls asking how one becomes a poet. She answered that it is a matter of innate talent, invoking the idea of the artist as genius. His lola, he shares, is remembered through awards from the local government to the National Historical Commission of the Philippines (NHCP.) When I ask if there is more work to be done, he evades the question. Reflecting on this moment, I consider how there is no pitiful story here, nothing to romanticize. The family is quite welcoming to students, researchers, and even fans of the poet deemed to be the rival of Jose Garcia Villa.==[x]== They have donated most of the matriarch's items to the Ateneo Library for Women's Writings (ALIWW), while seeking out funding for the restoration of the Bell Smith house. Yet it is a long-drawn-out ordeal that remains uncertain, and the family is aware of that. They relay that they had requested support from the local government, but until now, no hint of any restoration can be seen at the site.

Today, a faded marker from the NHCP memorializes the house of Manalang-Gloria. The Bell Smith milling company is foregrounded, and the poet is relegated to the margins. Everything fizzles out in the heat of the sleepy town, and the life of Manalang-Gloria dissolves into one of many here in Albay. 

The poet died shortly before I turned two years old. Her memory persists in conversation and in the constellation of people who once knew or remembered her. Tangentially, lingers in the communal memory of the city where this essay began.

**Coming Home**

In the quotidian scenes of [[Ecologies of Home/the market\|the market]], many of the workers are women. Manning a stall often falls to them. In one interview, I met a woman operating her stall with her partner; they moved and spoke with swift, practiced rhythm. The women of the Tabaco City Public Market inhabit their pace—some quietly, yielding to the slow rhythm of this coastal town, while others fill the space with chatter that animates the market.

In the quotidian scenes of the market, one finds that many who toil are women. In one of my interviews, I met a woman who operated the market stall with her partner who was also female. The two women moved and talked swiftly with the practiced rhythm of the market. The women of the Tabaco City Public Market occupy their space. Some do so quietly, yielding to the undemanding pace of this coastal city, while others fill the space with the chatter that  occasionally animates the market.

My lola once managed abaca production in the backyard of our family compound. She was born in 1928 in the small pueblo of Joroan. The daughter of a Filipina and a Chinese immigrant, my lola was a simple woman who was taught to be frugal as a child. She had married my lolo, the son of a lighthouse keeper from a small island off the coast of Ajuy, Iloilo. My lolo left home in his late teens, intending to study law. He ended up in Tabaco where he met my lola. There, in a coastal town, a child of the sea and a child of the mountains wed. As my lolo, who eventually became a lawyer, worked in the town hall as the Election Registrar, my lola, in her days off from teaching at a nearby public elementary school, invested some of their earnings in the growing abaca business of the 1950s. They needed the extra income to send their six children to school. As a family, they prayed to Nuestra Señora de Salvacion in the evenings and regularly went to church on Sundays.

In her abaca enterprise, my lola had employed others to wind [[Ecologies of Home/the fiber\|the fiber]] and produce the ropes. The land had auspiciously been bought from the Confesor family who had owned the entire parcel of land in Bombon. My lola's business would not last long, as the abaca production in the entire Bicol region dwindled. I sometimes wonder if my lola had known Angela Manalang-Gloria as Angela Buruka, and what she thought about the whole matter of a woman hardened by time and responsibility.

The mythic forces that define female existence in society hum beneath the surface and suffuse this discussion. Yet here, we return to the material concerns that do exist in Tabaco. The displacement of ecological knowledge echoes the transformation of the figure of the woman. Here, she is not the volcano, a witch, a serpent, or a saint. She is simply the lady waiting for a sale after sitting in the market for three days without having earned a cent.
 
This quiet labor mirrors how Albayanos approach the women in this essay. Nuestra Señora, while a figure of devotion and salvation, also occupies the most commonplace corners of the home, becoming an almost ubiquitous presence for the people of Albay. Magayon, looming as both volcano and the mythical woman, dwells in the same arena of collective consciousness. Hers is a story recited lazily, with a name uttered like a friend's: familiar and near at hand. For the people of Albay, Magayon never existed as a mythic being nor a grand figure. She simply exists as a testament to the tethering of women to nature, to the violation of their bodies, and to the infernos that orbit their stories--all within the domestic spaces that encroach upon the public realm, where lies a quiet resistance against disappearance and further wounding.
