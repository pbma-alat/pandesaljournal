As with much research, this one begins in the library. In Tabaco, the library is in the market. It settled there after the conflagration of 2003, and after the local government had decided to renovate the Arellano-designed city hall. The city is designed like all other Spanish colonial towns: in a grid, following the *bajo la campana* ideology of the colonial government. Here, the colonial languages bleed into one another and hat emerges is not only a spatial configuration but a way of moving and gathering that persists even as the material anchors of place are continually destroyed and remade.

![municipio 1929.jpg](/img/user/images/municipio%201929.jpg)

The neoclassical, columned façade of the stark white city hall looks out over the plaza. The plaza, a playground-cum-graveyard of public monumental sculpture, crawling with touristic significations of local identity, functions as a space of meeting and leisure for the city’s youth. The church plaza is a separate thing, practically a park meant to contain the spillover of churchgoers during Sunday masses. On a normal day, it sits empty, save for a few padyak drivers taking naps under the shade as they wait for the afternoon dismissal of nearby schools. Right in front of the plaza is the church of St. John the Baptist, built using massive blocks of black volcanic stone. In classic earthquake baroque architecture, the belfry is separated from the church. It sits closer to the highway, a graduated and stout bell tower that has been closed off to the public for some decades now. Carved into some of the stones are symbols of the religious order that oversaw the construction of the church, alongside the haphazardly etched names of stone masons and carpenters, all of which exist as quiet marks of labor and history.

In 1965, the Tabaco City market caught fire. Records of the fire exist in the town fiesta souvenir programs. Writing for the souvenir on June 9, 1965, the late Mayor Antonio A. Berces recounts, “When, in the early dawn of August 13, 1965, I viewed the havoc wrought by the conflagration which gutted our public market buildings, including private residences and commercial buildings, a prayer leaking through my lips, I stood there for some time with trepidation. But when, with forbearance and calm fortitude, you immediately commenced rebuilding from the smoldering ruins in our temporary market site, I took courage from you and firmly resolved that, like the mythical Phoenix bird of old, a modern, imposing, and beautiful market building shall rise from its ashes and out of its ruins.”

The kind Mayor Tony passed away a day later in Manila, while on a trip to secure funding for the reconstruction of the market from GSIS. He was succeeded by Mayor Apolonio Buban who, on June 13, 1965, also wrote about the market fire. “It has become a common characteristic of the Tabaquenos that even the dire consequences of calamity like the fire that recently ravaged the commercial and residential center of our town and the loss of one so dear, like the demise of a great, beloved and sincere leader, Mayor Tony Berces, may he now rest in peace, have not slackened our enthusiasm in celebrating our town fiesta in honor of our patron, Saint John the Baptist.”

The 1965 souvenir program is flooded with messages from different government officials responding to the market fire that the town had experienced. Many focused on the merit of sacrifice and on being “judicious in the spending of our financial and economic resources.” Diosdado Macapagal, also a Bicolano by blood, writes, “While I would not begrudge you your enjoyment, I should want to remind you that town fiestas should not be considered as an excuse for lavish spending; rather more they should serve to remind us of our individual responsibilities in the development of our community. In the midst of your celebration, may I remind you that the Administration is engaged in the great task of nation-building in which everybody is expected to contribute his share, however small. The sacrifices demanded of everyone are enormous, I know, but the rewards will certainly come if we are willing to pay the price in terms of patience and greater dedication to our task today.” All who wrote implored the Tabaqueños to turn to Divine Providence. Perhaps St. John the Baptist, the town’s patron saint, would conjure a miracle and provide a renewal of sorts.

In 2003, as Tabaco was just welcoming its third year as a city, another fire gutted the market. An eatery on the second floor was blamed for the fire, though in the aftermath, everyone’s grief was much too loud, and the slow, excruciating journey to rebuild weighed heavily on everyone’s minds. One of the market vendors remembers having been jostled awake by her father’s shouting. “Nasusulo su saod!” They had lived nearby, merely a few minutes’ walk away, but this time they ran. When they got there, they realized that in their hurry, they had forgotten to bring the key. By this time, the fire was growing fast, consuming the entire market until it was reduced to ash.

The market vendors were relocated by the town plaza, lining the outer walls of the local Catholic school. Others were transferred to Brgy. Cormidal, to the dismay of many because of how disorderly it was. Still, in these phases, the market was the market, the _saod_ was the _saod_. And in cases when the market was gone, we called wherever it was the _saodan_, or the place where one markets.

The new market was unveiled some months later. It embraced a new façade, a new layout, and a new set of tenants. There were those who could not source any capital to recover their old wares and let go of their businesses altogether.

They say there are more newcomers now at the market: outsiders coming from neighboring municipalities, _tagaluwas_, they are called. They maintain good relationships with each other, but old ties with old friends have perhaps dissipated. Other things, too, from the old market, like the organization of market vendors, with a representative for each section: the _siraan_, _karnehan_, and handicrafts, have disappeared. The organization used to represent the vendors in decision-making at the market. In the new market, many things have changed. Vendors were relocated to the second floor, while the ground floor is currently occupied by a grocery chain, a gadget store, and a couple of food chains. Some vendors share that they never really understood why the change was made. On the ground floor, they would be more visible to people. But having taken it up with the LGU, the engineers told them that they would be safer on the second floor, that it would keep them safe from flooding during typhoon season. No matter, they say as rainwater enters through the windows.

Today, sales are low and days are long. I came across one vendor who had not sold anything in three days. Others have the same story. Practically no one buys handicrafts anymore. Many places have embraced the craft and now sell these handicrafts all over the region, some even selling them outside Bicol. There is also the factor of online shopping, with people allegedly preferring to purchase products online, many of which originate from China. Some of them try to enter the online shopping circuit, but not many are able to. It demands time and manpower that they do not have. It is also crucial that not many tourists visit Tabaco City anymore. Without tourists, there are significantly fewer people buying these local products from sellers.

No one know how long they can sustain the business. Some would even be lucky if their children are interested in inheriting the business. The reconstruction of this market has promised renewal, but it is also through this that social and economic relations are rearranged. The same story is true of the library. There is a new building waiting for them a few minutes away from the sentro. The local government plans to relocate the library to the new legislative building along Ziga Ave. While the space is new and perhaps better equipped to preserve the books that sit in the humidity of the current location, it is also farther. Library staff are not enthused by the fact that the library is being taken away from the heart of the city, where they believe it is more accessible to students and other cityfolk. The library and its staff all exist now in liminality, as they ready themselves to face a rupture. As with the history of the market and the city, the library waits and hopes that they would be able to reconstruct relations that might be disrupted by this promised development.
