[[Ecologies of Home/By Flood and Fire - Ecologies of Home\|By Flood and Fire - Ecologies of Home]]

***An kalayo, an onos, an ciudad***

How many times must history be written?

This exhibit captures the relationships that surface within the ecologies of fire and deluge of Tabaco City, tracing their presence across myths and material reality. It reflects on these affinities as belonging simultaneously to the realms of injury and life, and explores how the city and its people continue to dwell within this dynamic. As these forces proffer life inasmuch as ruination, they also reveal the true complexities of our ecological relationships. Fire and deluge are approached with fear for their destructive nature, but also with a recognition of how they have shaped our history as a people. As in the story of Gugurang and Asuang, fire and deluge have aggressively constructed our environment, but our temperaments and culture, perhaps more quietly so.

The Bicolano brand of affect, at once fiery and propitiatory, is evidenced in our folklore and appears in our everyday cultural and social practices. The affective dimensions of fire and deluge roar in our language and palate, and hum in the confrontation of everyday worries. It resounds in the striking of metal against metal, and whirs in the turning of a wheel. These are all inscribed in the places we inhabit: the pandayan, the home, and, in some cases, the body. It emerges, too, in the market, as a space where the city gathers and one where, in a manner unique across places in the Philippines, the public library is also found.

Where we currently stand, one is confronted with two worlds that, while distinct, both serve as arenas of history and pathways of knowledge. One bears the weight of institutional memory while the other intimates histories from below. Nevertheless, both carry the marks of a community that lives through the cadence of the wind and waves, and continually inscribes the history of Tabaco City. As the library exists within the market, the market, too, transforms into a living archive of our city's ecology.

Anchored in the notion of the palengke as library, this exhibit traces the histories of the city through the Tabaco City Public Market and, concurrently, the Tabaco City Public Library. The building itself, where these two coexist, has been repeatedly singed by fire and lashed by rain, and stands as a material site of wounding and revival.

To walk through the market is to notice how the city gathers: the wares of the blacksmiths from the pandayans, abaca handicrafts, fish brought in from the port, rice harvested from fields across Albay, and the many expressions of local foodways. Each one faces a precarious relationship with rising heat and torrential rains, which continually interrupt their production. The palengke thus is further galvanized as the site where ecological forces are made visible as utterances of damage and subsistence.

The history of the city is the history of its people. This exhibit takes stock of these multifaceted ideations of our home and hopes to present new touchstones for recounting our city’s past while braving to imagine its future. It means to articulate an idea of history as something that occupies layered times and places. Some of the installations featured in the exhibit invite community participation to activate its growth into a living community archive for the people of Tabaco.

Ultimately, this exhibit frames places as worlds onto which we anchor our ways of doing, feeling, and knowing. It underscores the centrality of community in the continued breath of this ecology. In expounding on the cultural ecologies of Albay, this exhibit also turns inward, as it transforms into an act of a cultural worker’s self-reflection and auto-ethnography, all housed in a desire to spell out strategies for a decolonial curatorial practice.
